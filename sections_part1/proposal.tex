\subsection{Objectives and research hypotheses}
 

The accelerated nature of the expansion of our Universe remains one of the most 
mysterious puzzles in Physics, %\cite{riessObservationalEvidenceSupernovae1998,perlmutterMeasurementsOmegaLambda1999}, 
well consolidated by several recent and 
independent cosmological observations 
\cite{planckcollaborationPlanck2018Results2020,
broutPantheonAnalysisCosmological2022,
rubinUnionUNITYCosmology2025,
descollaborationDarkEnergySurvey2024,
desicollaborationDESIDR2Results2025}.
The expansion rate of the Universe $H(z)$ and the growth rate of the structures $f(z)$, 
both functions of cosmic time or redshift $z$, 
are two important observables that allow us to constrain dark energy models or alternatives to general relativity 
as potential solutions \cite{houCosmologicalProbesStructure2023}.%,hutererGrowthCosmicStructure2023}.
%\textbf{The main goal of \projacro\ is to produce robust measurements of the growth rate of structures
%at late cosmic times (when acceleration is taking place) by using state-of-the-art 
%observations of our Universe.}
The simplest model of \emph{dark energy} is a cosmological constant $\Lambda$, 
which can also be thought as an exotic fluid with an equation of state $w=-1$. 
More exotic dark energy models predict different values for $w$, or that it can evolve with redshift.  
Recent cosmological observations suggest that $w \neq -1$ at 
more than 3$\sigma$, pointing to an time-evolving dark energy 
\cite{desicollaborationDESIDR2Results2025}. 
Alternatively to dark energy, some models attempt to extend or modify general relativity 
to account for the acceleration, the so-called 
\emph{modified gravity theories}. %\cite{cliftonModifiedGravityCosmology2012,ishakTestingGeneralRelativity2019}.
To distinguish between dark energy and modified gravity models, it is essential to 
combine measurements of both expansion and growth rates \cite{ishakModifiedGravityConstraints2025}.

The growth rate $f(z)$ quantifies how fast initial perturbations of the cosmic density field, $\delta(\vec{x})$, 
grow with time or redshift $z$.   
It can be measured via the impact of \emph{peculiar velocities} of galaxies on the observed structures. 
Peculiar velocities modify redshifts due to the cosmic expansion. 
Both are generally undistiguishable, creating distortions in the 
observed galaxy density field, known as \emph{redshift-space distortions} (RSD). 
The two-point statistics of the galaxy density field, $\langle \delta(\vec{x}) \delta(\vec{y})\rangle$, 
becomes anisotropic w.r.t. the line-of-sight, allowing measurements of the growth rate $f(z)$ 
with spectroscopic galaxy surveys 
\cite{%alamClusteringGalaxiesCompleted2017,
alamCompletedSDSSIVExtended2021,
desicollaborationDESI2024FullShape2025}.
Moreover, if distance indicators are available for a large sample of galaxies,
it is possible to break the degeneracy between expansion and  
proper motion, yielding a radial velocity field $v(\vec{x})$.
Several distance indicators have been used
in recent years: 
the Tully-Fisher (TF) %, \cite{tullyNewMethodDetermining1977}) 
and Fundamental Plane (FP),% \cite{djorgovskiFundamentalPropertiesElliptical1987}) 
relations, as well as 
type-Ia supernovae (SNIa),% \cite{kowalAbsoluteMagnitudesSupernovae1968}). 
The first two indicators are abundant but yield more uncertain 
distances (about 20-30\%) than the last one (5-10\%), though less numerous.
%across the probed cosmic volume.

The two-point statistics of the velocity field, $\langle v(\vec{x}) v(\vec{y})\rangle$, 
also depends on $f(z)\sigma_8(z)$.
Measuring three combinations of two-point functions, i.e., 
density-density, velocity-velocity and their cross correlation
 $\langle \delta(\vec{x}) v(\vec{y})\rangle$,
yield tighter constraints than just one of such statistics 
\cite{qinRedshiftspaceMomentumPower2019,

to measure peculiar velocities and obtain the tightest constraints on the growth rate at late times.}

\subsection{Position of the project in relation to the state of the art}

%Given the high-quality and large statistical 
%power of such datasets, it is essential to develop precise and accurate methodologies 
%as well as careful tests and quantification of systematic uncertainties. 
The \projacro\ team has more than five years of expertise in this field and it is currently
well established as leads in key cosmological analyses with peculiar velocities, 
including surveys such as:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI):} 
  currently the best spectroscopic survey of galaxies to date, it will measure 
  63 million galaxy redshifts after eight years of observations (2021-2028) over 17,000~deg$^2$
  \cite{desicollaborationOverviewInstrumentationDark2022}.
  DESI galaxies are an excellent tracer of the distorted density field, 
  from which we can measure anisotropies due to RSD. 
  The DESI Peculiar Velocity survey will produce distance measurements to 
  53k spiral galaxies from the Tully-Fisher relation and 133k ellipticals 
  from the Fundamental Plane \cite{saulderTargetSelectionDESI2023},%,,saidDESIPeculiarVelocity2024,douglassDESIEDRCalibrating2025}.
  Those distances will yield a well sampled velocity field at $z<0.1$.
  
  \item \textbf{the Zwicky Transient Facility (ZTF):} 
  a photometric survey with large field of view and high cadence, ideal for the 
  discovery of transient events, including SNIa \cite{bellmZwickyTransientFacility2019}%,rigaultZTFSNIa2025}.
  Covering more than 17k~$deg^2$ of the Northen sky, perfectly overlapping with DESI
  observations, ZTF will discover more than 30k SNIa over redshifts $0 < z < 0.2$. 
  which will be used to obtain precise peculiar velocities.
\end{itemize}
\projacro\ will focus in the DESI and ZTF overlap, a perfect opportunity for cross-correlations and systematic cross-checks. 
Based on our own Fisher forecasts \cite{carreresGrowthrateMeasurementTypeIa2023,ravouxGeneralizedFrameworkLikelihoodbased2025}, 
\textbf{\projacro\ will produce a 8.3\% precision growth rate measurement when combining DESI galaxies and DESI+ZTF velocities 
(Figure~\ref{fig:fs8}), representing the most precise and robust estimate at late cosmic times. }

\projacro\ is organised in three connected work packages: synthetic datasets, 
systematic effects in real data, and cosmological implications of our results.

\subsubsection*{Work package 1: Synthetic datasets}

WP1 will focus on the production and analyses of realistic synthetic datasets 
reproducing properties of real data from DESI and ZTF. 

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Fast approximate simulations}
    Recent mock catalogs used for peculiar velocities are based on n-body simulations 
    such as AbacusSummit \cite{DESIPV_Bautista}%maksimovaABACUSSUMMITMassiveSet2021,DESIPV_Bautista}
    or Uchuu \cite{ishiyamaUchuuSimulationsData2021}, 
    which are high accuracy but are limited in volume or in the number of independent realisations.
    Using well-known techniques \cite{chuangEZmocksExtendingZel2015,zhaoCompletedSDSSIVExtended2021},
    we will  
    produce a large number ($O(1000)$) of independent realisations of density and velocity fields at low redshift. 
  
  \item \textbf{Production of synthetic DESI Peculiar velocity surveys and ZTF SNIa samples:} 
    We will add survey-like properties to the simulations above to create sythetic samples of DESI and ZTF surveys,
    including distance indicators.  
    %An important aspect of those mock catalogues is the fact that they originate from the same base simulations, 
    %so they include the cross-correlations between surveys. 
    For DESI, we will use existing infrastructure used to produce DESI Data Release 1 mock catalogues for TF and FP samples 
    \cite{DESIPV_Bautista}
    while for ZTF, we will build upon in-house experience \cite{carreresGrowthrateMeasurementTypeIa2023} 
    and use existing software (\textsc{skysurvey}) from the collaboration.  
    Those new mock catalogues will reach higher redshifts than current sets, 
    and will include subtle effects of redshift evolution and selection effects. 

   

  \item \textbf{Cross-covariance between methodologies and cosmological probes:}
    Several methodologies are able to exploit a distorted 
    density field and a peculiar velocity field 
    (e.g.  \cite{ravouxGeneralizedFrameworkLikelihoodbased2025,qinRedshiftspaceMomentumPower2025}
    ). 
    Those are commonly applied to the same initial dataset, 
    so results are correlated. 
    Using large sets of mock catalogues, we will 
    precisely estimate this cross-correlation, which is key to derive consensus results 
    (e.g. \cite{sanchezClusteringGalaxiesCompleted2017,
    bautistaCompletedSDSSIVExtended2021,
    dumerchatBaryonAcousticOscillations2022}
    ).
    The same mocks could be further improved to evaluate the cross-covariance 
    between growth measurements from peculiar velocities and weak lensing.
\end{itemize}


\subsubsection*{Work package 2: Systematic effects in real data}

Focusing on real data, WP2 will investigate potential systematic effects 
affecting growth measurements and will yield the final combined result from 
DESI and ZTF. 

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Quantify the impact of observational systematics:}
    With the mocks from WP1, 
    we will quantify for the first time the impact 
    of several observational systematics to an unprecedented level of accuracy.
    Several systematic effects can create spurious correlations in the observed velocity field, 
    potentially biasing our growth rate measurements \cite{rossCompletedSDSSIVExtended2020}. 
    We will pay particular attention to 
    the angular dependence of the Malmquist bias, dust attenuation, flux calibration residuals, 
    among others. 
    Strategies will be put in place to mitigate such impacts when possible, 
    otherwise a systematic uncertainty budget will be propagated to final results.

  \item \textbf{Growth rate measurement with a photometric sample of SNIa:}
    Building up on our experience with synthetic datasets of Rubin LSST SNIa
    \cite{rosselliForecastGrowthrateMeasurement2025a,carreresTypeIaSupernova2025}, 
    we will extend this work to the final release of ZTF SNIa data. 
    This photometrically-classified sample can potentially increase the 
    number of SNIa by factors of 5 to 6 compared to the spectroscopically-classified sample,
    also increasing the redshift range to $0< z< 0.2$.  

  \item \textbf{Cross-calibration of DESI and ZTF peculiar velocities:}
    Given the existence of three different distance indicators (TF, FP and SNIa) in the same 
    volume, there are many opportunities for cross-calibrations and consistency checks between 
    those, in regards to cosmological parameters. Cross-correlations between different surveys
    are much less sensitive to systematic effects, so measuring them adds to the robustness of
    our analysis. We aim at obtaining a final consensus growth rate measurement from this combination, 
    including all contributions of potential systematic errors. 

\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Work package 3: Cosmological implications of our measurements} 

WP3 will derive cosmological constraints from results of WP1 and WP2. 
Our growth rate measurement at low redshift,  
when combined with high redshift expansion and growth rate measurements (Fig.~\ref{fig:fs8}),
has the potential to improve constraints on dark energy and rule out some modified gravity theories.

The expansion rate will be provided by 
SNIa Hubble diagrams, baryon acoustic oscillations and the cosmic microwave background. 
At high redshift, DESI and Euclid will also provide precise growth rates from RSD. 
With those probes, WP3 will deliver constraints on:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\Omega_m$, $\sigma_8$, $H_0$ when assuming a $\Lambda$CDM model 
  \item $w_0$ and $w_a$ parameters describing the equation of state of evolving dark energy 
  \item $\gamma$ or $(\mu_0, \Sigma_0)$ which empirically parametrise deviations from general relativity. 
  In-house forecasts predict that the uncertainty on $\gamma$ reduces by a factor of $\sim 2$ 
  when including our low-redshift growth rate measurement. 
\end{itemize}
Those results will set the tightest constraints from the Northen sky. 
This work serve as basis for future analyses of datasets in the Southern sky such as 
Rubin LSST and 4MOST.
